> 「**Shàng yī yī guó, zhōng yī yī rén, xià yī yī bìng — The highest physician heals the state, the middle physician heals the person, the lowest physician heals the disease.**」
>
> ——Táng · Sūn Sīmiǎo, *Bèijí Qiānjīn Yàofāng · Lùn Zhěnhòu* (*Thousand Golden Prescriptions for Urgent Need · On Diagnosis*)

Many people think of Chinese medicine as nothing more than an **"old curio"** —
the needles in a museum, the herbs beside the stove, the family recipes of one's grandfather.

The truth, however, is far from this.

From the **late sixteenth century**, when Matteo Ricci described Chinese pulse-diagnosis to Europe in his *Notes on China*,
to the **seventeenth century**, when the Polish Jesuit **Michael Boym** published the *Flora Sinensis* in Vienna;
from the **1929** storm over **"Abolish Chinese Medicine (fèizhǐ zhōngyī, 废止中医)"**, which nearly severed Chinese medicine at its root in its native soil,
to the **2015** award of the Nobel Prize to **Tú Yōuyōu (屠呦呦)** for the artemisinin her work was inspired to discover by Gě Hóng's *Zhǒuhòu Bèijí Fāng*;
and on to the **2019** adoption of ICD-11 by the WHO, and the **2022** inscription of **"Traditional Medicine of China"** on the UNESCO Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity —

**The art of Qíhuáng, having come through storm and trembling, is now stepping calmly out onto the world stage.**

## I. The Late Míng and Qīng Periods: The Beginning of the "Western Transmission" of Chinese Medicine

### 📜 1. Matteo Ricci and the *De Christiana Expeditione apud Sinas* (1615)

**Matteo Ricci (利玛窦, 1552–1610)**, an Italian Jesuit missionary, arrived in China in 1582.
He spent 28 years in China, mastering the Chinese language, writing and publishing books.

His posthumous work, ***De Christiana Expeditione apud Sinas*** (*The Christian Expedition among the Sinas*, often translated into English as *Notes on China*),
was edited by Nicolas Trigault (金尼阁) and published in **1615** in Augsburg, Germany;
it was later translated into Chinese by Hé Gāojì, Wáng Zūnzhòng and Lǐ Shēn, and published by **Zhōnghuá Shūjú in 1983**.

In this book, Ricci, with the **wonder of an "outside observer"**,
for the first time **systematically introduced to Europe** the **pulse-diagnosis, medicinal substances and tongue-diagnosis of Chinese medicine**:

> 「**The Chinese have, in very many things, a most thorough study, especially in that branch of medical science which deals with the pulses. By means of the pulses they distinguish the various dispositions of the body, and the delicacy and subtlety of their observations in this matter are truly admirable.**」

This is **one of the earliest written Western records of Chinese pulse-study**.
Ricci did not dismiss Chinese medicine; rather, with a respectful pen, he recorded what he had seen of Chinese medicine.

> Source: Matteo Ricci, *De Christiana Expeditione apud Sinas*, Book I, Chapter 10; Chinese translation by Hé Gāojì et al., Zhōnghuá Shūjú, 1983.

### 📖 2. Michael Boym and the *Flora Sinensis* (1656)

**Michael Boym (卜弥格, 1612–1659)**, a Polish Jesuit missionary,
the scholar of the **"Stele of the Luminous Religion of Great Qin (大秦景教碑)"** at the court of the Southern Míng, was also the **earliest Westerner to introduce Chinese medicines to Europe**.

He published the ***Flora Sinensis*** (Vienna, **1656**),
which is **the first systematic European work on Chinese plants and medicines** —
the book described more than twenty Chinese medicinal substances, including **rhubarb (dàhuáng, 大黄), cassia bark (guìpí, 桂皮), lychee (lìzhī, 荔枝), lotus root (ǒu, 藕), lingzhi (língzhī, 灵芝), poria (fúlíng, 茯苓) and musk (shèxiāng, 麝香)**,
and was illustrated with woodcuts.

This was **the first time Chinese materia medica** had been presented to European readers **in the form of modern botany**,
and was cited by later naturalists including **Carl Linnaeus (Línnè)**.

> Source: *Flora Sinensis*, Michel Boym, Vienna, 1656;
> see also: Edward Kajdański, *Polish Jesuit and Pioneer of Sinology: Michael Boym*, Dàxiàng Publishing House.

### 🌍 3. Du Halde and the *Description de la Chine* (1735)

**Jean-Baptiste Du Halde (杜赫德, 1674–1743)**, a French Jesuit,
edited the Jesuit archives, and in **1735** published the four-volume ***Description de la Chine*** (*Description of the Chinese Empire*) in Paris.

The work contains a great deal of translated and introduced material on **Chinese pulse-studies, acupuncture, materia medica, and case-records**.
It influenced the **Enlightenment thinkers** of Europe such as **Voltaire** and **Montesquieu**,
and for the first time gave the European Enlightenment a **relatively systematic view of Chinese medicine**.

### 🩺 4. Acupuncture Becomes Fashionable in Nineteenth-Century Europe

In the early nineteenth century, **acupuncture** was brought into European medicine by French missionaries such as **Joachim Bouvet (Lùyī Báijìn)** and **Pierre Soulié**.
Around 1810, **French physicians** began to attempt acupuncture analgesia in clinical practice.
In **1822**, the English physician **James Morss Churchill** published ***A Treatise on Acupunturation***,
introducing acupuncture in a systematic way to the English-speaking world.

> A single silver needle, from then on, crossed the ocean and took root in Europe.

## II. Modern Times: Chinese Medicine Tossed in the Storm (1840–1949)

After the **Opium War**, Western medicine poured into China in great numbers, alongside missionaries, hospitals and medical schools.
For the first time Chinese medicine encountered a **genuine "opponent"**.

### ⚔️ 1. The Sino-Western Medical-Integration School — The Wisdom of "Seeking Common Ground while Respecting Differences"

Faced with the shock of Western medicine, a group of **Chinese-medicine forebears** from the late Qīng to the Republican period chose to **"exclude no one"**,
and tried to **integrate China and the West**, taking the strengths of each. This is the **"Sino-Western Medical-Integration School (Zhōngxīyī Huìtōng Xuépài, 中西医汇通学派)"**.

#### 🤝 Táng Zōnghǎi (1847–1897)

A native of Sìchuān, he authored the ***Zhōngxī Huìtōng Yījīng Jīngyì* (《中西汇通医经精义》, The Essential Meaning of the Medical Classics in Sino-Western Integration)** (published 1892) —
this is **the first Chinese medical monograph whose purpose is explicitly "Sino-Western integration"**.
Táng's guiding principle was: **"Do not keep to differences of boundary and land; only seek the middle path that converges in one truth" (不存疆域异同之见, 但求折衷归于一是).**

#### 🤝 Zhū Pèiwén (mid- to late 19th century)

A native of Guǎngdōng, he authored the ***Huáyáng Zàngxiàng Yuēzuǎn* (《华洋脏象约纂》, A Brief Compilation of Chinese and Foreign Visceral Manifestation)** (published 1892),
in which he **compared point by point** the viscera of the *Huángdì Nèijīng* with the **anatomy of Western medicine** —
this is **the first Chinese book to systematically compare Chinese and Western ideas about the viscera**.

#### 🤝 Yùn Tiěqiáo (1878–1935)

A native of Jiāngsū, he authored the ***Qúnjīng Jiànzhì Lù* (《群经见智录》, Records of Insight through the Many Classics)** (1922) and the ***Shānghán Lùn Yánjiū* (《伤寒论研究》, Studies in the Treatise on Cold-Damage)**.
He put forward the principle that **"Chinese medicine has real efficacy; one should seek the reasons of that efficacy from the efficacy itself" (中医有实效, 当从实效求其所以然)**,
refuting the simple negation that **"Chinese medicine is not scientific"**.

#### 🤝 Zhāng Xīchún (1860–1933)

A native of Héběi, he authored the ***Yīxué Zhōngzhōng Cānxī Lù* (《医学衷中参西录》, Records of Chinese-Western Medical Integration, in Loyalty to the Chinese and Reference to the Western)** in three series and thirty juǎn,
which is **the most complete and clinically practical monograph of the Integration School**.
His self-created **"Āsīpīlín Shígāo Tāng (Aspirin and Gypsum Decoction)"** (using the Western medicine aspirin together with the Chinese medicine gypsum)
is regarded as **an early model of Chinese-Western combined drug therapy**.

> 「**Zhōng zhōng zhě, gēnběn yě; cān xī zhě, fǔzhù yě — To be loyal to the Chinese is the root; to refer to the Western is the supplement.**」 —— Zhāng Xīchún
>
> Source: *Yīxué Zhōngzhōng Cānxī Lù*, by Zhāng Xīchún; Héběi People's Publishing House, 1957 revised edition.

### 🔥 2. The "Abolish Chinese Medicine" Storms (1912 & 1929)

The greatest tribulation suffered by Chinese medicine in modern times was the **two "institutional" crises of attempted abolition**.

#### ⚠️ The First: The 1912 "Omission of Chinese Medicine (Lòu Liè Zhōngyī Àn, 漏列中医案)"

In 1912, the Beiyáng government promulgated the **"New Educational Ordinances of the Republic of China"**,
**excluding Chinese medicine** from the **system of university education** —
this is the well-known **"Omission-of-Chinese-Medicine Case (Lòu Liè Zhōngyī Àn)"**.

This provoked **the first national petition of the Chinese-medicine community**,
with the Shénzhōu Chinese-Medicine Society and the Medical Association and other bodies submitting a joint letter to the Ministry of Education,
which finally forced the Beiyáng government **not to write the word "abolish" into the law**,
but the **effort to bring Chinese medicine into the official educational system failed**.

#### 🔥 The Second: The 1929 "Abolish-Old-Medicine Case (Fèizhǐ Jiùyī Àn, 废止旧医案)"

In **February 1929**,
the **Central Health Committee (Zhōngyāng Wèishēng Wěiyuánhuì, 中央卫生委员会)** of the Nationalist government held its first session,
and the sitting committee-member **Yú Yúnxiù (余云岫, also called Yú Yán, 余岩, 1879–1954)** proposed the
**"Proposal for the Abolition of Old Medicine as a Means of Removing the Obstacles to Medical and Health Affairs (废止旧医以扫除医事卫生之障碍案)"**,
arguing for **the gradual elimination of Chinese medicine over fifty years**.

Had this proposal passed, **Chinese medicine would have had no place to stand**.

#### 🚩 The "March 17" National-Medicine Demonstration

When the news leaked, the whole country was shocked.
On **17 March 1929**,
physicians and representatives from **17 provinces, 242 Chinese-medicine associations and 381 delegates** gathered in **Shànghǎi**,
to hold **the National Assembly of Medical-Profession Associations (Quánguó Yīyào Tuántǐ Dàibiǎo Dàhuì, 全国医药团体代表大会)**,
which chose **Xiè Guān (谢观), Lù Yuānléi (陆渊雷), Yùn Tiěqiáo (恽铁樵)** and others as representatives to go to Nánjīng to petition.
The Assembly afterwards fixed **17 March as "National-Medicine Day (Guóyī Jié, 国医节)"**.

After fierce resistance, the proposal **was ultimately not passed**.
But the **legal status, the right to run schools and the right to hold examinations of Chinese medicine**
remained, for many years afterwards, in a precarious state of storm and trembling.

> Source: *Yījiè Chūnqiū* (*Medical Spring-and-Autumn Journal*, 1926–1937, ed. Zhāng Zànchén), issues 34–39;
> Dèng Tiétāo, chief ed., *Zhōngguó Yīxué Tōngshǐ · Jìndài Juǎn* (*General History of Chinese Medicine · Modern Volume*), People's Medical Publishing House, 2000.

> A branch of medicine, branded as "old medicine" in its own native land — what a piece of absurdity, and what a piece of solemn tragedy.

## III. The New China: The "Phoenix-Nirvana" of Chinese Medicine (1949 to the present)

### 🌟 1. After 1949: "Chinese Medicine and Pharmacology Is a Great Treasure-House"

After **1949**, the fate of Chinese medicine was **fundamentally reversed**.

> 「**Zhōngyīyào xué shì yīgè wěidà de bǎokù, yīngdāng nǔlì fājué, jiāyǐ tígāo — Chinese medicine and pharmacology is a great treasure-house; we should spare no effort to unearth it, and raise it to a higher level.**」
>
> ——Mao Zedong, instruction of **11 October 1958** on the Report of the Party Group of the Ministry of Health on the Summary of the Western-Medicine-Leave-and-Study-Chinese-Medicine Classes.

- **In 1950**: the First National Health Work Conference established the policy of **"uniting Chinese and Western medicine (tuánjié zhōngxīyī, 团结中西医)"**
- **In 1956**: **Běijīng College of Chinese Medicine (北京中医学院)**, **Shànghǎi College of Chinese Medicine (上海中医学院)**, **Guǎngzhōu College of Chinese Medicine (广州中医学院)** and **Chéngdū College of Chinese Medicine (成都中医学院)** were established in succession,
  and Chinese medicine entered the **modern higher-education system** for the first time
- **"Chinese-Western medical integration (zhōngxīyī jiéhé, 中西医结合)"** became **one of the basic policies of the national health work**

> Source: archives of the Ministry of Health of the People's Republic of China; *Zhōngguó Yīxué Tōngshǐ · Xiàndài Juǎn* (*General History of Chinese Medicine · Modern Volume*), People's Medical Publishing House, 2000.

### 🏆 2. Tú Yōuyōu and Artemisinin (2015 Nobel Prize)

**Tú Yōuyōu (屠呦呦, born 1930)**, a native of Níngbō, Zhèjiāng, and a researcher at the China Academy of Chinese Medical Sciences,
was the recipient of the **2015 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine**,
and the **first scientist from mainland China to receive a Nobel Prize in the natural sciences**.

The inspiration for her prize-winning discovery came from a **Chinese-medicine classic of seventeen hundred years ago** —

> 「**Qīnghāo yī wò, yǐ shuǐ èr shēng zì, jiǎo qǔ zhī jìn fú zhī — A handful of qīnghāo (sweet wormwood), soaked in two shēng of water, wring out the juice and drink it all.**」
>
> ——Eastern Jìn · Gě Hóng, *Zhǒuhòu Bèijí Fāng · Zhì Hánrè Zhū Nüè Fāng* (c. 340 CE)

In 1969, **Tú Yōuyōu** was appointed head of the **"523 Project (Project 523)" (the search for new anti-malaria drugs)**.
She was inspired by the *Zhǒuhòu Bèijí Fāng*'s use of the character **"zì (渍, soak in cold water)"** rather than **"jiān (煎, decoct in hot water)"**,
and in **1971** successfully extracted **artemisinin (qīnghāosù, 青蒿素)**;
in **1972** the clinical verification was completed.

The **artemisinin-class drugs have saved millions of malaria patients worldwide**,
and the **World Health Organization (WHO)** lists them as **"the first-line treatment for malaria in the 21st century"**.

> Source: Tú Yōuyōu, "Artemisinin — A Gift from Traditional Chinese Medicine to the World", *Nature* special article, 2011; Nobel Prize official website announcement, 2015.
>
> **This is the greatest contribution of Chinese medicine to world medicine.**

### 📚 3. WHO Recognition: The Inclusion of Traditional Medicine in ICD-11 (2019)

On **25 May 2019**, the **72nd World Health Assembly (WHA72)** in Geneva adopted the
**International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems, 11th Revision (ICD-11)**.

**This is the first time in the more than one-hundred-year history of the ICD that "traditional medicine" has been included** —
**Chapter 26 of ICD-11** sets up a special section on **"Traditional Medicine Conditions"**,
and records **150 traditional-medicine disease-terms**,
covering the systems of **Chinese medicine, acupuncture, and Ayurveda**, among others.

> 「**ICD-11 enables, for the first time, the counting of traditional medicine services and encounters.**」——WHO official website.

**ICD-11 formally took effect on 1 January 2022**.

> Source: WHO, *ICD-11 Introduction and Chapter 26*;
> Resolution WHA72.15 of the 72nd World Health Assembly (May 2019).

### 🏛️ 4. UNESCO List: "Traditional Medicine of China" (2022)

From **28 November to 3 December 2022**,
the **17th session of the Intergovernmental Committee for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage** of the **United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO)**
was held in **Rabat, Morocco**.

The session inscribed **"Traditional Medicine of China" (中国传统医药)**
on the **Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity (人类非物质文化遗产代表作名录)**.

This is **the 43rd** item from China to be inscribed on a UNESCO list,
and is **the highest-level cultural-identity recognition** that **Chinese medicine** has received at the international level.

> Source: UNESCO ICH official website ich.unesco.org,
> No. 01965, Traditional Medicine of China, Inscribed 2022.

## IV. Chinese Medicine Overseas: Going Out onto the World Stage

> 「**Táolǐ bù yán, xià zì chéng xī — Where peach and plum trees bloom, a path is made beneath them of itself.**」 —— *Shǐjì · Lǐ Jiāngjūn Lièzhuàn* (*Records of the Grand Historian · Biography of General Li*)

### 🏯 1. Japan: Kampō Medicine (汉方医学, Kanpō)

Chinese medicine, **transmitted to Japan from the Suí and Táng dynasties onward**, was localised and gave rise to **"Kampō Medicine" (汉方医学, Kanpō)**.

- **148 Kampō preparations** are included in the **Japanese national health insurance** (Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare, *Standard of Kampō Preparations as Pharmaceutical Products*)
- The number of **long-term users of Kampō medicines** reaches **millions**
- **In 1976**: the **"Japan Society of Oriental Medicine"** was founded, later reorganised as the **"Japan Society of Kampō Medicine (日本汉方医学会)"**
- **In 2012**: the **"Japan Society of Chinese Medicine (日本中医学会)"** was founded, and exists alongside the Japan Society of Kampō Medicine

> Source: Japanese Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare, *Standard of Kampō Preparations as Pharmaceutical Products*; Yakazu Dōmei (矢数道明), *A History of Kampō Medicine*.

### 🌏 2. Korea: Korean Medicine (한의학, Hániuìhak)

After Chinese medicine was transmitted to the Korean peninsula, it gave rise to **"Korean Medicine" (한의학, Korean: Hániuìhak)**.
Korea now has **eleven Korean-medicine universities** (statistics of the late 2010s),
and **"Korean Medicine"** is **part of the legal medical system of Korea**, running in parallel with Western medicine.

### 🩺 3. Europe and America: From Rejection to Recognition

#### 🇺🇸 The United States: The Legalisation of Acupuncture (1970s–1997)

- **In 1971**: the journalist **James Reston** published in *The New York Times* an account of his personal experience of **post-operative acupuncture analgesia**, sparking wide public attention in the United States
- **In 1973**: the State of **Nevada** became the first to **legalise acupuncture**
- **In November 1997**: the **U.S. National Institutes of Health (NIH)** convened the
  **Acupuncture Consensus Statement (NIH Consensus Statement)** conference,
  **formally acknowledging the efficacy of acupuncture for a number of conditions** —
  this was the **first** time the official United States had **endorsed acupuncture**
- To this day, **acupuncture is recognised in all 50 U.S. states**, and Chinese-medicine clinics are to be found in major cities such as **New York, San Francisco, and Los Angeles**

> Source: NIH Office of Alternative Medicine, *Acupuncture Consensus Statement*, 1997.

#### 🇪🇺 Europe: From France to Britain

- **France**: the earliest country in Europe to accept acupuncture; in the 1820s it was already being trialled in hospitals
- **The United Kingdom**: the *Traditional Herbal Medicinal Products Regulations* were passed in 2016
- **Germany**: from 2015, certain acupuncture treatments have been included in the statutory health insurance

### 🌍 4. The Global Map: More Than 180 Countries and Regions

According to **public data of the World Health Organization**,
**Chinese medicine has now spread to more than 180 countries and regions of the world**,
and the **number of people worldwide who have used Chinese medicine runs into the billions**.

> Source: WHO Traditional Medicine Strategies 2014–2023 and 2019–2023, who.int.

## V. Why Qíhuáng Study Has Written This Essay

> 「**Bù zhī láilù, bù chéng qùlù — If one does not know the road by which one came, one cannot form the road by which to go.**」

In writing this long essay, **it is not out of nostalgia for the past**,
but **to let every reader who reaches this point know three things** —

### 🌱 1. Chinese Medicine Is Not an "Old Curio"

Chinese medicine is a **living medical system** that is **still being studied, still being verified, still being accepted by the world**.
From the birth of the *Flora Sinensis* in Vienna in 1656,
to artemisinin's reception of the Nobel Prize in Stockholm in 2015,
to the adoption of ICD-11 in Geneva in 2019 —

**Chinese medicine has never stopped "going abroad", and it has never stopped "growing up".**

### 🌏 2. Chinese Medicine Is an Important Part of the Soft Power of Chinese Culture

Chinese medicine is **not an isolated "medicine"**; it is a
**composite of astronomy, geography, meteorology, the observation of natural phenomena, philosophy and ethics** —
it carries with it the Eastern thinking of **"Heaven-human unity (tiānrén héyī, 天人合一)"**, **"the harmony of Yin and Yang (yīnyáng héhé, 阴阳和合)"** and **"treatment according to the individual (yīn rén zhì yí, 因人制宜)"**,
and is **a unique contribution of Chinese civilisation to the world**.

### 🪶 3. Qíhuáng Study: A "Sincerity" That Is Backed by Sources

In every piece of content on **Qíhuáng Study (岐黄书房)** —
- **Quotations from classical works** are always marked with *Classic · Chapter*
- **Modern data** are always marked with the institution and the literature
- **Contested content** is always marked with "one school says (一说)" or "according to the record (据载)"

**We do not set ourselves up as a self-praising "divine physician"; we are only a "cultural translator" backed by sources.**

## VI. The "Five Gates" through Which Qíhuáng Must Pass to Go Abroad

If we liken the process of Chinese medicine's "going abroad" to the voyage of a ship,
then it **must pass through five gates** —

### 🚪 The First Gate: The Gate of Language (16th–18th c.)

From Ricci, Boym and Du Halde, to the missionary-physicians of the 19th century —
**to translate the Chinese medical texts into Latin, French and English.**

### 🚪 The Second Gate: The Gate of Theory (19th c.)

From anatomy, physiology and pharmacology —
**to re-read "qì and blood (气血)", "meridians (经络)" and "Yin-Yang (阴阳)" within the framework of modern science.**

### 🚪 The Third Gate: The Gate of Institution (20th–21st c.)

From the storm over "Abolish Chinese Medicine", to the establishment of the colleges of Chinese medicine, to WHO recognition —
**to make Chinese medicine a "legitimate medicine", and not a "folk experience".**

### 🚪 The Fourth Gate: The Gate of Evidence (20th–21st c.)

From artemisinin to the evidence-base of acupuncture —
**to prove the efficacy of Chinese medicine through modern randomised controlled trials (RCTs).**

### 🚪 The Fifth Gate: The Gate of Culture (21st c.)

From ICD-11 to the UNESCO list —
**to let Chinese medicine step onto the world stage in the double identity of "medicine + culture".**

> 「**Qíhuáng zhī dào, jì gǔ yì jīn; jì zhōng yì xī; jì shēn yì xīn; jì shù yì dào — The Way of Qíhuáng is at once ancient and modern; at once Chinese and Western; at once bodily and spiritual; at once art and Way.**」
