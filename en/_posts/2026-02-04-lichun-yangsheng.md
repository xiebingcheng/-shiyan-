>
> ——《黄帝内经·素问·四气调神大论》

> "The three months of spring are the season of *fā chén* (发陈, the outpouring of what is stored). Heaven and Earth together give birth; the ten thousand things flourish. Go to bed late, rise early; stride through the courtyard; let the hair hang loose and loosen the body, so that the *zhì* (志, will) may be born. Be generative and do not kill; be giving and do not take; be rewarding and do not punish. This is correspondence with the qi of spring, and the way of *yǎng shēng* (养生, nurturing life)."
>
> — *Huángdì Nèijīng* (《黄帝内经》, Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon), *Sùwèn* (《素问》, Basic Questions), *Sìqì Tiáoshén Dàlùn* (《四气调神大论》, Great Treatise on Regulating the Spirit with the Four Qi)

Lichun (立春, Beginning of Spring) is the **first of the twenty-four solar terms (二十四节气, èrshísì jiéqì)** — the opening of the four seasons, when all things are made new.

When the handle of the Big Dipper points east, spring has come to the world. The ancients regarded Lichun as the "head of the year" (岁首, suìshǒu): the Son of Heaven would personally lead his ministers to greet spring at the eastern altar; common folk would whip the clay ox, paste the character "春" (chūn, spring) on their doors, and "bite the spring pancake" (咬春, yǎo chūn) — all to welcome this first wisp of newly born yang.

## I. The Three Pentads of Lichun (立春三候)

The *Yuèlìng Qīshí'èr Hòu Jíjiě* (《月令七十二候集解》, Monthly Ordinances and the Seventy-Two Pentads Explained) records:

- **First pentad — The east wind thaws the ice (东风解冻):** The east wind is born of spring; it disperses the cold of winter.
- **Second pentad — The hibernating insects begin to stir (蛰虫始振):** Buried creatures wake, but have not yet emerged.
- **Third pentad — The fish rise, bearing the ice (鱼陟负冰):** Fish deep in the water gradually rise, swimming beneath the thawing ice.

Across these three pentads runs a single character: **"movement" (动, dòng)** — ice breaking, insects stirring, fish swimming. In caring for human life, we too should follow this principle of "moving" and "being born" (生, shēng).

## II. Daily Routine: Retire Late, Rise Early, Stride through the Courtyard

The *Nèijīng* (《内经》, Inner Canon) is explicit: in spring one should **"retire late and rise early" (夜卧早起, yè wò zǎo qǐ)**.

"Retiring late" here does not mean staying up late; going to sleep around the *hài* hour (亥时, 21:00–23:00) is enough. "Rising early" means getting up around the *mǎo* hour (卯时, 05:00–07:00), hair loose, body relaxed, walking slowly through the courtyard — letting body and mind awaken together with the spring yang.

To avoid:

- ❌ Lying in bed and refusing to rise — the rise of yang qi (阳气, yángqì) is suppressed, which most damages the spring's birth-force.
- ❌ Tying the hair tightly, wearing constricting clothes — when the body is bound, the qi (气, qì) cannot flow freely.
- ❌ Staying shut indoors all day — without wind and sun, the qi becomes weak and disease follows.

Recommended: on rising, comb the hair a hundred strokes; walk a hundred slow steps; stretch the sinews; let a faint sweat appear, then stop.

## III. Diet: Reduce Sour, Increase Sweet — Nurture the Spleen, Support the Liver

> "In the months of spring, eat less sour and more sweet." (春月少酸宜食甘)

Sour enters the liver (酸味入肝) and is normally a flavor that follows the liver's nature. Yet in spring, liver qi (肝气, gānqì) is already running high; too much sour causes **liver qi to become hyperactive and restrain the spleen earth (肝气过亢，反克脾土, gānqì guò kàng, fǎn kè pítǔ)**. Therefore in spring one should **reduce sour and increase sweet (减酸增甘, jiǎn suān zēng gān)** — eat less vinegar and plums, drink less sour-plum decoction; eat more Chinese yam (山药, Shānyào), jujube (大枣, Dàzǎo), Job's tears (薏米, Yìmǐ), glutinous rice (糯米, Nuòmǐ), and oats (燕麦, Yànmài).

**The "Three Treasures of Lichun" (立春三宝)**:

1. **Spring pancakes (咬春, yǎo chūn, "biting the spring")** — thin pancakes rolled with scallion, sauce, and tender chives (韭菜, Jiǔcài). Pungent and sweet together transform into yang, supporting the rise of yang qi.
2. **Spinach (菠菜, Bōcài)** — nourishes blood and moistens dryness; the "red-mouthed green parrot" of spring vegetables, the very picture of the season.
3. **Radish (萝卜, Luóbo) — and especially the radish leaves (萝卜缨, Luóboyīng)** — pungent, sweet, slightly cool; they move qi and aid digestion, preventing spring lethargy.

A **gǒuqǐ-shānyào porridge (枸杞山药粥)** can serve as a week's worth of breakfast through Lichun:

Dice 50 g of Chinese yam; add 10 goji berries (枸杞, Gǒuqǐ) and an appropriate amount of millet (小米, Xiǎomǐ); cook together until congee forms. Chinese yam tonifies the spleen and boosts the kidney; goji nourishes the liver and brightens the eyes; millet nurtures stomach qi. These three flavors are mild and balanced, suitable for old and young alike.

## IV. Emotions: Be Generative, Do Not Kill; Be Giving, Do Not Take

Spring belongs to wood (木, mù) and corresponds to the liver; the liver governs **"shū xiè" (疏泄, free coursing and discharge)**, and is associated with the emotion of **anger (怒, nù)**.

In spring one should be **expansive, generous, and giving** —

- **"Be generative, do not kill" (生而勿杀):** cultivate and nurture, not destroy; do not casually invoke force, do not casually cut off life.
- **"Be giving, do not take" (予而勿夺):** give and share more; do not quarrel with others, do not keep score.
- **"Be rewarding, do not punish" (赏而勿罚):** encourage and praise more; criticize and repress less.

It is said that **"the plan of the year begins in spring" (一年之计在于春)**. If the spring of body and mind is not well tended, the whole year easily slides downhill.

## V. The Meridians: Tap the Gallbladder Meridian, Press Tàichōng (LV-3)

After Lichun, the daily practice of **tapping the Gallbladder Meridian (胆经, Dǎnjīng)** and **pressing the Tàichōng point (Tàichōng, LV-3, 太冲穴)** is recommended:

**Tapping the Gallbladder Meridian (敲胆经):**
On the outer side of the thigh, from the hip joint to the knee, along the line of the trouser seam, make a fist and tap — 200 strikes per side.
The Gallbladder Meridian is the *shào yáng* (少阳, lesser yang) vessel; it governs the body's qi of "springing forth" (生发之气, shēngfā zhī qì). Tapping it can invigorate yang.

**Pressing Tàichōng (揉太冲):**
On the dorsum of the foot, in the depression two finger-widths above the web between the great toe and the second toe.
Use the thumb to press and knead, 3 minutes per side, **until a sore, distended sensation is reached (至酸胀为度, zhì suān zhàng wéi dù)**.
Tàichōng is the *yuán xué* (原穴, source point) of the Liver Meridian; pressing it can soothe the liver, resolve constraint (疏肝解郁, shūgān jiěyù), and calm and subdue liver fire (平降肝火, píngjiàng gānhuǒ).

In the month after Lichun, "spring lethargy" (春困, chūnkùn) reaches its peak.
If you often feel tired on waking, drowsy, and lacking appetite, yang qi is failing to rise smoothly.
At such a time, the worst things to do are **lying in, oversleeping, and prolonged sitting**; what helps is moving, being cheerful, and walking.

## Conclusion

Lichun health preservation, in the final analysis, comes down to a single character — **"shēng" (生, generation; life)**.

- In daily routine, **generate** and **do not** bind;
- In diet, **generate** yang and **do not** over-sour;
- In emotions, **generate** harmony and **do not** harbor anger;
- In the meridians, **generate** flow and **do not** stagnate.

Grasp this character "shēng," and you grasp the full merit of spring.
May all of you, after Lichun, enjoy body and mind at peace — a year that is, throughout, spring.
