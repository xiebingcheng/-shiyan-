> 「**Huángdì yuē:…… Qí bó yuē:…… — The Yellow Emperor said:…… Qíbó answered:……**」

> 「**Qínqiú gǔxùn, bócǎi zhòngfāng — Diligently I sought the ancient teachings, broadly I gathered the many prescriptions.**」

> ——Eastern Hàn · Zhāng Zhòngjǐng, Preface to the *Shānghán Zábìng Lùn*

If we liken **Chinese medicine** to a **hall a thousand years old**,
then the **Four Great Classics** are **the four giant pillars that hold this hall up**.

They are:

- 🟢 **The *Huángdì Nèijīng* (《黄帝内经》, The Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon)** — **establishes the theory (lǐ)**
- 🔵 **The *Nánjīng* (《难经》, The Classic of Difficult Issues)** — **unties the difficulties (nán)**
- 🟠 **The *Shānghán Zábìng Lùn* (《伤寒杂病论》, Treatise on Cold-Damage and Miscellaneous Diseases)** — **sets the methods (fǎ)**
- 🟣 **The *Shénnóng Běncǎo Jīng* (《神农本草经》, The Divine Farmer's Classic of Materia Medica)** — **establishes the medicines (yào)**

Taken together, **theory, method, formula and medicine are all complete**,
and the **art of Qíhuáng** at last **stretches forth in magnificent array**.

## I. Prologue: What Is Meant by "The Four Great Classics"

### 1. The "Four Great Classics" Is Not a Phrase from the Original Antiquity

The phrase **"the Four Great Classics of Chinese Medicine (中医四大经典)"** does **not** come from **the pre-Qín and the Hàn periods**;
it is a **summary** reached by **later generations of physicians** after **long study and clinical verification**.
It is generally held that this phrase **matured during the Míng and Qīng periods**,
and was **formally established** in the **modern Chinese-medicine educational system**.

Since the Republican period, the running of Chinese-medicine schools (such as the predecessor of the **Shanghai University of Traditional Chinese Medicine (上海中医药大学)**, the **Shanghai School of Chinese Medicine (上海中医专门学校)**, and the predecessor of the **China Academy of Chinese Medical Sciences (中国中医科学院)**)
has placed the ***Nèijīng*, the *Nánjīng*, the *Shānghán*, the *Jīnkuì* and the *Běncǎo*** on the **required-foundation list**,
and from this the term **"Four Great Classics (四大经典)"** became a **convention accepted by use**.

### 2. The Four Classics Acknowledged Today

> 「**Lǐ bì *Nèijīng*, fǎ bì *Shānghán*, yào bì *Běnjīng*, nán bì *Nánjīng* — For theory, the *Nèijīng*; for method, the *Shānghán*; for medicines, the *Běnjīng*; for difficulties, the *Nánjīng*.**」

The **four classics** acknowledged in contemporary Chinese-medicine scholarship are:

| No. | Title | Nature | Brief Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| 🟢 | **The *Huángdì Nèijīng*** | **Ancestor of theory** | The foundational work of the **theoretical system** of Chinese medicine |
| 🔵 | **The *Nánjīng*** | **Classic of clarifying the classics** | Expounds the **difficulties** of the *Nèijīng* |
| 🟠 | **The *Shānghán Zábìng Lùn*** | **Ancestor of prescription books** | Source of **pattern differentiation and treatment** and of the **classical formulas (jīngfāng, 经方)** |
| 🟣 | **The *Shénnóng Běncǎo Jīng*** | **Source of materia medica** | The **earliest extant** monograph of materia medica |

### 3. "The Four Together Reveal the Whole"

The four books are **by no means isolated**.
**The *Nèijīng* sets up the theory (lǐ); the *Nánjīng* explains the theory;**
**The *Běnjīng* sets up the medicines (yào); the *Shānghán* sets up the methods (fǎ)** —
**theory, method, formula and medicine (lǐ-fǎ-fāng-yào, 理法方药)** run through them **as one thread**.
Hence **Xú Língtāi (徐灵胎)** said:

> 「**《Běncǎo》《Líng》《Sù》, shèngjīng yě; 《Shānghán》《Yàolüè》, xiánlùn yě; xiánlùn yóu kě cānwù, shèngjīng bùróng yī zì lǔmǎng — The *Běncǎo*, the *Líng* and the *Sù* are sacred classics; the *Shānghán* and the *Yàolüè* are the discussions of sages. The sage's discussions may still be reflected on and fathomed, but in the sacred classics not a single character may be handled carelessly.**」

——*Yīxué Yuánliú Lùn · Jīngdiǎn Lùn* (*On the Origins and Flow of Medicine · On the Classics*)

## II. The *Huángdì Nèijīng* — The Ancestor of Chinese-Medical Theory

### 1. Composition and Structure

The **Huángdì Nèijīng (《黄帝内经》, The Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon)** is **not the work of any one person at any one time**;
it was **completed between the Warring States and the Qín and Hàn dynasties (c. 5th c. BCE – 1st c. CE)**,
a **collection of essays** long **compiled, edited and supplemented** by **numerous physicians**.

The book is divided into **two parts**, **eighty-one chapters** each, totalling **162 chapters**:

| Part | Number of Chapters | Main Contents |
|---|---|---|
| **The *Sùwèn* (《素问》, Basic Questions)** | **81 chapters** | **Medical theory, visceral manifestation, cause of disease, mechanism of disease, therapeutic principles** |
| **The *Língshū* (《灵枢》, The Spiritual Pivot)** | **81 chapters** | **Meridians, acupuncture, needling methods, acupoints (shùxué, 腧穴)** |
| **Total** | **162 chapters** | **The foundation of Chinese-medicine theory** |

The **earliest catalogue entry** appears in the ***Hànshū · Yìwén Zhì***:
> 「**《Huángdì Nèijīng》 shíbā juǎn, 《Sùwèn》 jí qí jīng zhī jiǔ juǎn yě, jiān 《Língshū》 jiǔ juǎn, nǎi qí shù yān — The *Huángdì Nèijīng* is eighteen juǎn; the *Sùwèn* is nine juǎn of it, and together with nine juǎn of the *Língshū* that makes the number.**」

——*Hànshū · Yìwén Zhì · Fāngjì Lüè* (*Book of Hàn · Treatise on Literature and the Arts · Summary of the Technical Arts*)

### 2. Core Thought (1) — Yin-Yang and the Five Elements

> 「**Yīnyáng zhě, tiāndì zhī dào yě, wànwù zhī gāngjì, biànhuà zhī fùmǔ, shēngshā zhī běnshǐ, shénmíng zhī fǔ yě, zhìbìng bì qiú yú běn — Yin and Yang are the Way of Heaven and Earth, the great cord and net of the ten thousand things, the father and mother of change, the root and beginning of life and death, the dwelling of the spirit-lights; in treating disease one must seek the root.**」

——*Sùwèn · Yīnyáng Yìngxiàng Dàlùn* (*Basic Questions · The Great Treatise on the Correspondences of Yin and Yang*)

> 「**Mù shēng huǒ, huǒ shēng tǔ, tǔ shēng jīn, jīn shēng shuǐ, shuǐ shēng mù — Wood gives birth to fire, fire gives birth to earth, earth gives birth to metal, metal gives birth to water, water gives birth to wood.**」

——*Sùwèn · Wǔyùnxíng Dàlùn* (*Basic Questions · The Great Treatise on the Five-fold Movement*)

**Yin-Yang** is the **great cord**; **the five elements (wǔxíng, 五行)** are the **model**.
The two combined form **the most fundamental world-view and methodology of Chinese medicine**.

### 3. Core Thought (2) — The Theory of Visceral Manifestation (zàngxiàng, 藏象)

> 「**Xīn zhě, jūnzhǔ zhī guān yě, shénmíng chū yān.**
> **Fèi zhě, xiāngfù zhī guān, zhìjié chū yān.**
> **Gān zhě, jiāngjūn zhī guān, móulǜ chū yān.**
> **Dǎn zhě, zhōngzhèng zhī guān, juéduàn chū yān.**
> **Dànzhōng zhě, chénshǐ zhī guān, xǐlè chū yān.**
> **Píwèi zhě, cānglǐn zhī guān, wǔwèi chū yān.**
> **Dàcháng zhě, chuándào zhī guān, biànhuà chū yān.**
> **Xiǎocháng zhě, shòushèng zhī guān, huàwù chū yān.**
> **Shèn zhě, zuòqiáng zhī guān, jìqiǎo chū yān.**
> **Sānjiāo zhě, juédú zhī guān, shuǐdào chū yān.**
> **Pángguāng zhě, zhōudū zhī guān, jīnyè cáng yān, qìhuà zé néng chū yǐ — The heart is the office of the sovereign; the bright spirit issues from it.**
> **The lung is the office of the chancellor; management and order issue from it.**
> **The liver is the office of the general; strategy and deliberation issue from it.**
> **The gallbladder is the office of the upright middle; decision and judgement issue from it.**
> **The dànzhōng (pericardial centre) is the office of the envoys; joy and pleasure issue from it.**
> **The spleen and stomach are the offices of the granaries; the five flavours issue from them.**
> **The large intestine is the office of transmission; change and transformation issue from it.**
> **The small intestine is the office of reception; transformation of things issues from it.**
> **The kidney is the office of the strong worker; skill and cleverness issue from it.**
> **The sānjiāo (triple burner) is the office of drainage; the waterways issue from it.**
> **The bladder is the office of the district capital; the fluids are stored in it, and by qì transformation they can come out.**」

——*Sùwèn · Línglán Mìdiǎn Lùn* (*Basic Questions · Treatise on the Secret Code of the Lín-lán Hall*)

The **theory of visceral manifestation (zàngxiàng, 藏象)** is the Chinese-medicine **holistic view centred on the five zàng** —
**the heart governs the blood and vessels, the liver governs the free coursing, the spleen governs the transport and transformation, the lung governs qì, the kidney governs water** —
all of which were **established here**.

### 4. Core Thought (3) — The Theory of the Meridians (jīngluò, 经络)

> 「**Jīngmài zhě, suǒyǐ néng jué sǐshēng, chǔ bǎi bìng, tiáo xūshí, bùkě bù tōng — The meridians are what can determine life and death, treat the hundred diseases, and regulate vacuity and repletion; they must be thoroughly understood.**」

——*Língshū · Jīngmài* (*The Spiritual Pivot · The Meridians*)

The *Língshū* discusses in detail the **twelve regular meridians** (**three yīn of the hand, three yáng of the hand, three yīn of the foot, three yáng of the foot**),
the **eight extraordinary vessels (qíjīng bāmài, 奇经八脉)** (**Dū, Rèn, Chōng, Dài, Yīnwéi, Yángwéi, Yīnqiāo, Yángqiāo**),
the **fifteen luò-connecting vessels (biéluò, 别络)**, the **twelve jīngbié (经别)**, and others,
forming **the theoretical foundation of Chinese acupuncture**.

### 5. Core Thought (4) — Cause of Disease and Mechanism of Disease (bìngyīn bìngjī, 病因病机)

> 「**Zhèngqì cún nèi, xié bùkě gān; xié zhī suǒ còu, qí qì bì xū — When upright qì is stored within, evil cannot intrude; where evils gather, the qì must be vacuous.**」

——*Sùwèn · Cìfǎ Lùn* (*Basic Questions · Treatise on the刺法 (needling methods)*)

> 「**Fēngyǔ hánrè, bùdé xū, xié bùnéng dú shāng rén. Cùrán féng jífēng bàoyǔ ér bù bìng zhě, gài wú xū, gù xié bùnéng dú shāng rén — Wind, rain, cold, heat — if there is no vacuity, evil cannot harm a person alone. Those who meet with sudden violent wind and torrential rain and yet do not fall ill have no vacuity; therefore evil cannot harm them alone.**」

——*Língshū · Bǎi Bìng Shǐ Shēng* (*The Spiritual Pivot · The Generation of the Hundred Diseases*)

This line of thought **remains, even today, the central principle of Chinese-medicine life-nurturance** —
**support the upright and dispel the evil (fúzhèng qūxié, 扶正祛邪); avoid the evil and nurture the upright (bìxié yǎngzhèng, 避邪养正)**.

### 6. Core Thought (5) — Treating the Not-Yet-Manifest Disease (zhì wèi bìng, 治未病)

> 「**Shìgù shèngrén bù zhì yǐ bìng zhì wèi bìng, bù zhǐ yǐ luàn zhì wèi luàn, cǐ zhī wèi yě. Fú bìng yǐ chéng ér hòu yào zhī, luàn yǐ chéng ér hòu zhì zhī, pì yóu kě ér chuān jǐng, dòu ér zhù zhuī, bù yì wǎn hū — Therefore the sage does not treat disease once it has already arisen, but treats it before it arises; he does not treat disorder once it has already broken out, but treats it before it breaks out — this is what is meant. To take medicine after the disease has already formed, to govern only after the disorder has already broken out, is like digging a well when one is thirsty, or forging a spear-head when the battle has already begun — is it not too late?**」

——*Sùwèn · Sìqì Tiáoshén Dàlùn* (*Basic Questions · The Great Treatise on the Regulation of the Spirit in the Four Qì*)

The three characters **"zhì wèi bìng (治未病, treat the not-yet-manifest disease)"** constitute **one of the greatest contributions of Chinese medicine to world medicine**.
The three-tiered preventive idea —
**prevent before disease arises (wèi bìng xiān fáng, 未病先防), prevent change when disease has already arisen (jì bìng fáng biàn, 既病防变), prevent recurrence after recovery (chà hòu fáng fù, 瘥后防复)** —
antedated the West **by more than two thousand years**.

### 7. Core Thought (6) — The Five-fold Movement and the Six Qì (wǔyùn liùqì, 五运六气)

> 「**Fú wǔyùn yīnyáng zhě, tiāndì zhī dào yě, wànwù zhī gāngjì, biànhuà zhī fùmǔ, shēngshā zhī běnshǐ, shénmíng zhī fǔ yě, kě bù tōng hū — The five-fold movement and Yin-Yang are the Way of Heaven and Earth, the great cord and net of the ten thousand things, the father and mother of change, the root and beginning of life and death, the dwelling of the spirit-lights; how can they be left ununderstood?**」

——*Sùwèn · Tiānyuánjì Dàlùn* (*Basic Questions · The Great Treatise on the Origin of the Celestial*)

The doctrine of the **five-fold movement and the six qì (wǔyùn liùqì, 五运六气)**, in short the **"yùnqì (运气)"**,
is a **special doctrine of Chinese medicine** that **studies the influence of climatic change on disease**,
and is **the fount of Chinese chronomedicine (shíjiān yīxué, 时间医学)**.

### 8. Historical Status and Modern Research

The **Huángdì Nèijīng** has been honoured as **"the Ancestor of Qíhuáng (Qíhuáng zhī zōng, 岐黄之宗)"** and **"the Sage of the Medical Lineage (yījiā zhī shèng, 医家之圣)"**.
In the **Táng dynasty** it was placed on the **"must-read for physicians"** list;
in the **Sòng dynasty** Lín Yì and others of the **Medical Books Correcting Bureau (Jiàozhèng Yījú Shū, 校正医书局)** collated it;
in the **Míng dynasty** Zhāng Jǐngyuè (张景岳) commented upon it comprehensively in his ***Lèi Jīng* (《类经》, Classified Canon)**.

In **2011**, the project of **"Archives of Chinese Traditional Medicine (中国传统医药档案)"**
(jointly submitted by the **National Library of China (中国国家图书馆)** and the **China Academy of Chinese Medical Sciences (中国中医科学院)** and comprising nine classical works including the *Huángdì Nèijīng* and the *Běncǎo Gāngmù*)
was inscribed by **UNESCO (United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization)** on the **Memory of the World Register (世界记忆名录)**,
marking the fact that **the Chinese-medicine classics** have received **the highest international level of documentary-heritage recognition**.

> Source: Official page of the UNESCO Memory of the World Register;
> related announcement by the National Administration of Traditional Chinese Medicine, 2011.

## III. The *Nánjīng* — Untieing the Difficulties of the *Nèijīng*

### 1. Composition and Author

The **Nánjīng (《难经》, The Classic of Difficult Issues)**, full title **"Huángdì Bāshíyī Nánjīng" (《黄帝八十一难经》, The Yellow Emperor's Eighty-One Difficult Issues)**,
was **completed in the late Eastern Hàn dynasty (c. 1st–2nd c. CE)**.
Tradition attributes it to **Biǎn Què (Qín Yuèrén)**,
but in fact this is a **fictitious attribution** — this is the **"pseudepigraphic tradition (wěituō, 伪托)"** commonly found in Chinese medical classics,
whose purpose is to **borrow the authority of antiquity to spread the Way**.
The modern consensus holds that the *Nánjīng* was edited and compiled by a **certain anonymous physician of the late Eastern Hàn dynasty**.

### 2. Structure: Eighty-one Difficulties

The whole book is **written in the question-and-difficulty form**:
it cites the *Nèijīng*'s original meaning with **"jīng yún (经云, the Classic says)"**,
and answers with **"rán (然, thus)"**,
making altogether **eighty-one questions and answers**, whence the name **"Bāshíyī Nán (八十一难, Eighty-One Difficulties)"**.

| Range of Difficulties | Main Content |
|---|---|
| **1–22 Nán** | **Pulse studies (màixué, 脉学)** (the method of taking the cùnkǒu alone) |
| **23–29 Nán** | **Meridians**, **needling methods** (yíng suí bǔ xiè, 迎随补泻 — "迎随" tonifying-draining) |
| **30–47 Nán** | **Visceral manifestation (zàngxiàng, 藏象)** (form and physiology of the zàng-fu) |
| **48–61 Nán** | **Cause of disease**, **mechanism of disease**, **needling methods** |
| **62–68 Nán** | **Five-shū points, yuan-source points, needling methods** |
| **69–81 Nán** | **Tonifying-draining with the needle**, **miscellaneous discussions of needling** |

### 3. Core Thought (1) — Taking the Cùnkǒu Alone (dúqǔ cùnkǒu, 独取寸口)

> 「**Shí'èr jīng jiē yǒu dòngmài, dú qǔ cùnkǒu, yǐ jué wǔzàng liùfǔ sǐshēng jíxiōng zhī fǎ, hé wèi yě?**
> **Rán: Cùnkǒu zhě, mài zhī dàhuì, shǒu tàiyīn zhī mài dòng yě…… — The twelve meridians each have their moving vessel; to take the cùnkǒu alone, in order to decide the life-and-death and good-or-evil of the five zàng and six fǔ — what does this mean?**
> **Thus: The cùnkǒu is the great meeting of the vessels; it is where the vessel of the hand's greater yīn moves……**」

——*Nánjīng · Yī Nán* (*Classic of Difficult Issues · Difficulty One*)

This is **the greatest revolution in Chinese pulse diagnosis** —
previously, physicians had to **palpate the vessels of the twelve meridians in turn (as described in *Sùwèn · Sānbù Jiǔhòu Lùn*, *Basic Questions · The Treatise on the Three Sections and the Nine Indicators*)**,
but the **Nánjīng** created **"taking the cùnkǒu alone"**,
relying on the **cùn, guān and chǐ of the two hands — six positions in all** —
**to investigate the entire body's five zàng and six fǔ**.
This pulse-taking method **has been in continuous use to this day**, and has become the **"standard equipment" of Chinese pulse diagnosis**.

### 4. Core Thought (2) — The Left Kidney and the Right Mìngmén (Mìngmén, 命门)

> 「**Qí zuǒ zhě wéi shèn, yòu zhě wéi mìngmén. Mìngmén zhě, jīngshén zhī suǒ shè, yuánqì zhī suǒ xì yě, nánzǐ yǐ cángjīng, nǚzǐ yǐ xìbāo — The one on the left is the kidney; the one on the right is the mìngmén. The mìngmén is the dwelling of the spirit and the root to which the original qì is tied; in men it stores the jīng (essence); in women it ties the bāo (uterus).**」

——*Nánjīng · Sānshíliù Nán* (*Classic of Difficult Issues · Difficulty Thirty-Six*)

The term **"mìngmén (命门, Gate of Destiny)"** was here **systematised for the first time** —
the doctrine of **"left kidney, right mìngmén"** laid the foundation for later **"Mìngmén doctrine (mìngmén xuéshuō, 命门学说)"** developed by physicians such as **Zhāng Jǐngyuè (张景岳)** and **Zhào Xiànkě (赵献可)**.

### 5. Core Thought (3) — Yíng-Suí Tonifying and Draining (yíng suí bǔ xiè, 迎随补泻)

> 「**Déqì, yīn tuī ér nèi zhī, shì wèi bǔ; dòng ér shēn zhī, shì wèi xiè — Once qì is obtained, to push and insert is called "tonifying" (bǔ); to move and lift is called "draining" (xiè).**」

——*Nánjīng · Qīshíbā Nán* (*Classic of Difficult Issues · Difficulty Seventy-Eight*)

The principle of **"yíng suí bǔ xiè"** —
**"meeting" (yíng, 迎) the direction in which the meridian qì comes to drain (xiè);**
**"following" (suí, 随) the direction in which the meridian qì goes to tonify (bǔ)** —
this is **the core principle of acupuncture tonification and drainage**.

### 6. Core Thought (4) — The Five-shū Points and the Yuán-source Points

> 「**Jǐng zhǔ xīnxià mǎn, yíng zhǔ shēnrè, shū zhǔ tǐzhòng jié tòng, jīng zhǔ chuǎnké hánrè, hé zhǔ nìqì ér xiè — The jǐng (well) point masters fullness below the heart; the yíng (spring) point masters body-heat; the shū (stream) point masters heaviness of body and pain in the joints; the jīng (river) point masters panting, cough, cold and heat; the hé (uniting) point masters counterflow qì and diarrhoea.**」

——*Nánjīng · Liùshíbā Nán* (*Classic of Difficult Issues · Difficulty Sixty-Eight*)

The **rule of the indications of the five-shū points (jǐng, yíng, shū, jīng, hé)**
and the **establishment of the twelve yuán-source points (yuánxué, 原穴)**
are all **major contributions of the Nánjīng to acupuncture studies**.

### 7. Historical Status

> 「**《Nánjīng》…… rán qí shuō hónghé, xī yǔ 《Nèijīng》 xiāng fāmíng, ér wéi zhōngyī zhī guīniè — The *Nánjīng*…… its teachings, broad and expansive, are in full accord with the *Nèijīng* and bring it forth into the light; it is the standard and rule of Chinese medicine.**」

——Huá Bórén (滑伯仁), *Nánjīng Běnyì* (*Original Meaning of the Classic of Difficult Issues*)

The **Nánjīng** has been praised by later generations as **"the Classic among the Classics (jīng zhōng zhī jīng, 经中之经)"** and **"one of the Four Great Classics"**,
and it is **an important bridge that carries on the Nèijīng and gives rise to the Shānghán**.

### 8. Editions

The main editions are:

- **The Yītǒng Edition (医统本)** (Míng · Wáng Jiǔsī, ed.) — the most widely transmitted
- **The *Nánjīng Běnyì* (《难经本义》, Original Meaning of the Classic of Difficult Issues)** (Yuán · Huá Shòu, annotated) — the most refined in its commentary
- The **Gǔyì (古义, Ancient Meaning)** and **Jízhù (集注, Collected Annotations)** editions, among other multi-commentator recensions

## IV. The *Shānghán Zábìng Lùn* — The Ancestor of Pattern Differentiation and Treatment

### 1. The Author: Zhāng Zhòngjǐng

**Zhāng Zhòngjǐng (张仲景)**, personal name **Zhāng Jī (张机)**,
a famous medical scholar of the **Eastern Hàn dynasty**,
**born c. 150 CE, died 219 CE**,
a native of **Nányáng Commandery (南阳郡)** (present-day **Nányáng, Hénán**), in later generations honoured as **"the Medical Sage (Yīshèng, 医圣)"**.
According to the *Míngyī Lù* (*Records of Famous Physicians*) by the Táng-dynasty Gān Bózōng,
he had once served as **Governor of Chángshā (长沙太守)**, so that later generations also called him **"Zhāng Chángshā (张长沙)"** (this claim is **still debated in modern scholarship**).

> **Note**: Neither the *Hòu Hànshū* (*Book of the Later Hàn*) nor the *Sānguó Zhì* (*Records of the Three Kingdoms*) **erected a biography for Zhāng Zhòngjǐng**;
> the main sources for his life are later documents such as **the Preface to the *Zhēnjiǔ Jiǎyǐ Jīng* by Jìn-dynasty Huángfǔ Mì**, **the *Míngyī Lù* by Táng-dynasty Gān Bózōng**, and **Sòng-dynasty Lín Yì's collation of the *Shānghán Lùn***.

### 2. The Reason for Writing: Moved by Heaven, Pitying Humankind

> 「**Yú zōngzú sù duō, xiàng yú èrbǎi. Jiàn'ān jìnián yǐlái, yóu wèi shí rěn, qí sǐwáng zhě, sān fēn yǒu èr, shānghán shí jū qí qī. Gǎn wǎngxī zhī lúnsàng, shāng héngyāo zhī mò jiù, nǎi qínqiú gǔxùn, bócǎi zhòngfāng, zhuànyòng 《Sùwèn》《Jiǔ Juǎn》《Bāshíyī Nán》《Yīnyáng Dàlùn》《Tāilú Yàolù》, bìng píngmài biànzhèng, wèi 《Shānghán Zábìng Lùn》 hé shíliù juǎn — My clan was originally numerous, in all about two hundred. Since the first year of the Jiàn'ān era, not yet ten years, of those who died, two in three, and of those seven in ten died of cold-damage. Moved by the fall of those who had passed away, grieved that those cut off in untimely death could not be rescued, I diligently sought the ancient teachings, broadly gathered the many prescriptions, made use of the *Sùwèn*, the *Jiǔ Juǎn*, the *Bāshíyī Nán*, the *Yīnyáng Dàlùn*, the *Tāilú Yàolù*, together with the level-pulse pattern-differentiation, and composed the *Shānghán Zábìng Lùn* in sixteen juǎn.**」

——Preface to the *Shānghán Zábìng Lùn*

In a brief **hundred characters**, **each word is blood and tears** —
of his clan of two hundred people, **in less than ten years two-thirds died**,
and **seven in ten of them died of "cold-damage (shānghán, 伤寒)"** (in ancient usage, "cold-damage" referred broadly to **externally-contracted heat diseases (wàigǎn rèbìng, 外感热病)**).
Zhòngjǐng, **"moved by the fall of those who had passed away, grieved that those cut off in untimely death could not be rescued"**,
**diligently sought the ancient teachings and broadly gathered the many prescriptions**,
and **poured out his grief in writing**.

### 3. Transmission and Division into Two Books

The **Shānghán Zábìng Lùn** was originally **sixteen juǎn**,
but was **partly lost in the turmoil at the end of the Eastern Hàn dynasty**.
The **Western Jìn** Director of the Imperial Medical Office **Wáng Shūhé (王叔和)** (c. 201–280 CE) **gathered and edited it**,
dividing it into **two books**:

| Title | Juǎn | Content | Nature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The *Shānghán Lùn* (《伤寒论》, Treatise on Cold-Damage)** | **10 juǎn** | Externally-contracted heat diseases (**six-channel pattern differentiation**) | Treating **external contractions** |
| **The *Jīnkuì Yàolüè* (《金匮要略》, Essential Prescriptions of the Golden Chamber)** | **6 juǎn** | Internal injury miscellaneous diseases (**miscellaneous-disease treatment**) | Treating **internal injury** |
| **Total** | **16 juǎn** | **External + internal** | **A complete clinical system** |

> Hence later generations **jointly call them** **"the Shānghán and Jīnkuì"** or **"the Complete Works of Zhòngjǐng (Zhòngjǐng Quánshū, 仲景全书)"**.

### 4. Core Thought (1) — Six-channel Pattern Differentiation (liùjīng biànzhèng, 六经辨证)

> 「**Tàiyáng zhī wéi bìng, mài fú, tóuxiàng jiàngtòng èr hán — Tàiyáng disease: floating pulse, stiffness and pain of the head and nape, and aversion to cold.**」

——*Shānghán Lùn · Biàn Tàiyáng Bìng Mài Zhèng Bìng Zhì Shàng* (*Treatise on Cold-Damage · Pattern Differentiation of Tàiyáng Disease, Pulse, Pattern and Treatment, Part One*)

The **six channels** are **Tàiyáng (Greater Yang), Yángmíng (Bright Yang), Shàoyáng (Lesser Yang), Tàiyīn (Greater Yin), Shàoyīn (Lesser Yin), Juéyīn (Absolute Yin)** —
rooted in the *Sùwèn · Rè Lùn* (*Basic Questions · Treatise on Heat*), but **creatively developed**:
they are not only **six meridians**,
but also **six levels, six stages, six groups of patterns (zhènghòuqún, 证候群)**.
The **three cords (sāngāng, 三纲) of surface, interior and half-surface-half-interior**,
the **four eyes (sìmù, 四目) of cold, heat, vacuity and repletion**,
the **three pivots (sānjī, 三机) of opening, closing and hinging**,
together give rise to **397 methods and 112 (or 113) formulas**, later abbreviated as **"397 methods, 113 formulas"**.
**151 medicinal substances** are used.

### 5. Core Thought (2) — Pattern Differentiation and Treatment (biànzhèng lùnzhì, 辨证论治)

> 「**Guān qí màizhèng, zhī fàn hé nì, suí zhèng zhì zhī — Observe the pulse and the pattern, perceive which reversal has been committed, and treat according to the pattern.**」

——*Shānghán Lùn · Biàn Tàiyáng Bìng Mài Zhèng Bìng Zhì Zhōng* (*Treatise on Cold-Damage · Pattern Differentiation of Tàiyáng Disease, Pulse, Pattern and Treatment, Part Two*)

The four characters **"biànzhèng lùnzhì (辨证论治, pattern differentiation and treatment)"** are **the soul of Chinese medicine** —
not **"biànbìng lùnzhì (辨病论治, disease differentiation and treatment)"**, not **"biànyīn lùnzhì (辨因论治, cause differentiation and treatment)"**,
but **"guān qí màizhèng (observe the pulse and the pattern)"** — **on the basis of the present pattern**, **formulate method and prescribe formula**.
This is **the most fundamental clinical thinking of Chinese medicine, distinguishing it from Western medicine**.

### 6. Classical Prescriptions

Among the **113 formulas** of the *Shānghán Lùn*, **several are truly the "crown of all formulas"**:

| Formula | Source | Indication | Monarch (jūn) Herbs |
|---|---|---|---|
| 🟢 **Guìzhī Tāng (Cinnamon Twig Decoction)** | *Shānghán Lùn* | **Tàiyáng wind-stroke (汗出恶风, sweating with aversion to wind)** | Guìzhī (cinnamon twig), Báisháo (white peony) |
| 🟢 **Máhuáng Tāng (Ephedra Decoction)** | *Shānghán Lùn* | **Tàiyáng cold-damage (无汗而喘, no sweat with panting)** | Máhuáng (ephedra), Guìzhī (cinnamon twig) |
| 🟢 **Xiǎo Cháihú Tāng (Minor Bupleurum Decoction)** | *Shānghán Lùn* | **Shàoyáng disease (寒热往来, alternating cold and heat)** | Cháihú (bupleurum), Huángqín (scutellaria) |
| 🟢 **Wǔlíng Sǎn (Five-Ingredient Powder with Poria)** | *Shānghán Lùn* | **Tàiyáng water-accumulation (小便不利, difficulty of urination)** | Fúlíng (poria), Zhūlíng (polyporus) |
| 🟢 **Shènqì Wán (Kidney-qì Pill)** | *Jīnkuì Yàolüè* | **Kidney-yáng vacuity (腰膝酸软, soreness of the lower back and knees)** | Fùzǐ (aconite), Guìzhī (cinnamon twig) |

**Guìzhī Tāng** has been praised as **"the crown of all formulas (qúnfāng zhī guàn, 群方之冠)"** —
**it nourishes yīn and harmonises yáng, harmonises the camp and the guard (yíngwèi, 营卫)**,
and is the formula with the **greatest number of variations** (**Guìzhī Jiā Gégēn Tāng, Guìzhī Jiā Hòupò Xìngzǐ Tāng, Guìzhī Jiā Lónggǔ Mǔlì Tāng**……).

### 7. Historical Status

> 「**Zhòngjǐng zhě, yī zhōng zhī shèng yě; qí fāng, zé gǔjǐn fèng wéi guīniè — Zhòngjǐng is the sage among physicians; his formulas, ancient and modern alike, are held up as the standard and rule.**」

Later generations have honoured the *Shānghán Zábìng Lùn* as **"the Ancestor of Prescription Books (fāngshū zhī zǔ, 方书之祖)"** and **"the Ancestor of the Classical Formulas (jīngfāng zhī zōng, 经方之宗)"**.
In the **Sòng dynasty** Lín Yì, Sūn Qí and others of the **Medical Books Correcting Bureau (校正医书局)** collated it,
and fixed it as the **"Sòng edition of the *Shānghán Lùn*" (Sòngběn 《Shānghán Lùn》)** (re-carved in the Míng dynasty by **Zhào Kāiměi (赵开美)**, hence called the **"Zhào kè běn (赵刻本, Zhào-carved edition)"**),
which **has been transmitted down to the present day**.

### 8. Lineage of Transmission

The number of later physician-scholars and schools that have studied the *Shānghán Lùn* runs into the **hundreds**;
the **most prominent** include:

- **The "Errata-and-Re-edition School (Cuòjiǎn Chóng-dìng Pài, 错简重订派)"** — **Fāng Yǒuzhí (方有执)**, **Yù Chāng (喻昌)**
- **The "Preserve-the-Old-Text School (Wéihù Jiùlùn Pài, 维护旧论派)"** — **Zhāng Qīngzǐ (张卿子)**, **Zhāng Zhìcōng (张志聪)**
- **The "Pattern-Differentiation-and-Treatment School (Biànzhèng Lùnzhì Pài, 辨证论治派)"** — **Kē Qín (柯琴)**, **Xú Dàchūn (徐大椿)**
- **The "Formula-Pattern-Correspondence School (Fāngzhèng Duìyìng Pài, 方证对应派)"** — **Yoshimasu Tōdō (吉益东洞)** (**Japan**)
- **In the modern era** — **Hú Xīshū (胡希恕)**, **Liú Dùzhōu (刘渡舟)**, **Lǐ Péishēng (李培生)**, **Hǎo Wànshān (郝万山)**, among others

The **jīngfāng (classical formulas, 经方)** remain, even today, **the central weapon of the Chinese-medicine clinic**.

## V. The *Shénnóng Běncǎo Jīng* — The Source of Materia Medica

### 1. Composition and Author

The **Shénnóng Běncǎo Jīng (《神农本草经》, The Divine Farmer's Classic of Materia Medica)**, abbreviated as the ***Běnjīng* (《本经》)**,
was **completed in the late Eastern Hàn dynasty (c. 1st–2nd c. CE)**,
with an **unknown author**.
It is neither **Shénnóng** (a **legendary figure**),
nor any one specific **identified physician**,
but should rather be regarded as a **collective creation**, **summarised and brought together by many physicians** in the course of **long clinical practice**.
That it **bears the borrowed name of "Shénnóng"** is just as in the case of the *Nèijīng*'s **borrowing the name "Huángdì"** —
**to borrow the name of a sage, in order to spread it far**.

### 2. Structure: 365 Medicinal Substances, Divided into Three Grades

The whole book records **365 medicinal substances**,
**corresponding to the 365 days of the year** (**"fǎ sānbǎi liùshíwǔ dù, yī dù yìng yī rì, yǐ chéng yī suì — following the three-hundred-and-sixty-five degrees, one degree answering one day, to make a complete year"**).
**Divided into upper, middle and lower three grades**:

| Grade | Number of Substances | Nature | Principle of Use | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 🟢 **Upper Grade (shàngpǐn, 上品)** | **120 substances** | **Non-toxic**, governs nurturing of destiny and corresponds to Heaven | **May be taken long-term** (taking much and for long does not harm the person) | Rénshēn (ginseng), Gāncǎo (licorice), Dàzǎo (jujube), Gǒuqǐ (Lycium berry), Ējiāo (donkey-hide gelatin) |
| 🟠 **Middle Grade (zhōngpǐn, 中品)** | **120 substances** | **Some toxic, some non-toxic**, governs nurturing of nature and corresponds to humans | **Use with discernment** | Dāngguī (Chinese angelica), Máhuáng (ephedra), Sháoyào (peony), Dānshēn (salvia), Gégēn (kudzu) |
| 🔴 **Lower Grade (xiàpǐn, 下品)** | **125 substances** | **Mostly toxic**, governs treatment of disease and corresponds to Earth | **May not be taken long-term** | Dàhuáng (rhubarb), Fùzǐ (aconite), Bànxià (pinellia), Bādòu (croton), Ézhú (zedoary) |
| **Total** | **365 substances** | | | |

### 3. Core Contribution (1) — The Four Qì and the Five Flavours (sìqì wǔwèi, 四气五味)

> 「**Yào yǒu suān, xián, gān, kǔ, xīn wǔwèi, yòu yǒu hán, rè, wēn, liáng sìqì — Medicines have five flavours: sour, salty, sweet, bitter, acrid; and also four qì: cold, hot, warm, cool.**」

——*Shénnóng Běncǎo Jīng · Xùlì* (*The Divine Farmer's Classic of Materia Medica · Prefatory Remarks*)

The **"four qì (sìqì, 四气)"** (**cold, hot, warm, cool**) and the **"five flavours (wǔwèi, 五味)"** (**sour, bitter, sweet, acrid, salty**),
are **the foundation of the pharmacology of Chinese medicine**.
**Cold and cool treat yáng heat; warm and hot treat yīn cold**;
**Acrid disperses, sour astringes, sweet slows, bitter hardens, salty softens** —
**each of the five flavours has its use**.

### 4. Core Contribution (2) — Channel-Entry (guījīng, 归经) and the Monarch–Minister–Assistant–Envoy (jūnchén-zuǒshǐ, 君臣佐使)

> 「**Yào yǒu jūn, chén, zuǒ, shǐ, yǐ xiāng xuānshè héhé — Medicines have monarch, minister, assistant and envoy, in order to proclaim, restrain, combine and harmonise one another.**」

——*Shénnóng Běncǎo Jīng · Xùlì* (*The Divine Farmer's Classic of Materia Medica · Prefatory Remarks*)

The **"jūnchén-zuǒshǐ (君臣佐使, monarch–minister–assistant–envoy)"** —
this is **the basic structure of formula-construction in Chinese medicine**.
It was later developed into the **"zhǔ-fǔ-zuǒ-shǐ (主辅佐使, chief–deputy–assistant–envoy)"** of the ***Sùwèn · Zhìzhēn Yào Dàlùn*** (*Basic Questions · The Great Treatise on the Subtleties of the Supreme*),
which stands in **direct continuity with the *Běnjīng*'s teaching**.

### 5. Core Contribution (3) — The Seven Relations (qīqíng, 七情)

> 「**Yào yǒu dānxíng zhě, yǒu xiāngxū zhě, yǒu xiāngshǐ zhě, yǒu xiāngwèi zhě, yǒu xiāngshā zhě, yǒu xiāngwù zhě, yǒu xiāngfǎn zhě. Fáncǐ qīqíng, héhé shì zhī — Medicines have those that go alone, those that mutually need, those that mutually employ, those that mutually fear, those that mutually kill, those that mutually dislike, those that mutually oppose. All these seven relations, in combination and harmony, observe them.**」

——*Shénnóng Běncǎo Jīng · Xùlì* (*The Divine Farmer's Classic of Materia Medica · Prefatory Remarks*)

| The Seven Relations | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Single action (dānxíng, 单行)** | One medicinal alone is effective (e.g. **Dúshēn Tāng, Single-Ginseng Decoction**) |
| **Mutual need (xiāngxū, 相须)** | Two medicines of similar performance, used together to enhance effect (**Shígāo + Zhīmǔ**) |
| **Mutual employment (xiāngshǐ, 相使)** | Chief and auxiliary combine, the auxiliary enhancing the chief (**Huángqí + Fúlíng**) |
| **Mutual fear (xiāngwèi, 相畏)** | One medicinal restrains the toxicity of another (**Shēngjiāng wèi Bànxià — bànxià fears shēngjiāng**) |
| **Mutual killing (xiāngshā, 相杀)** | One medicinal eliminates the toxicity of another (**shēngjiāng kills the toxicity of bànxià**) |
| **Mutual dislike (xiāngwù, 相恶)** | Two medicines, used together, weaken each other's action (**rénshēn dislikes láifúzǐ — although one school says this is not in fact the case**) |
| **Mutual opposition (xiāngfǎn, 相反)** | Used together they can produce severe toxicity (**the source of the "Eighteen Incompatibilities" (shíbā fǎn, 十八反) and the "Nineteen Mutual-Fears" (shíjiǔ wèi, 十九畏)**) |

The **prohibition-theory of the "Eighteen Incompatibilities" and the "Nineteen Mutual-Fears"** has had its **foundation laid from this point on**.

### 6. Core Contribution (4) — "The Place of Earth" from Which Medicines Come

> 「**Tǔdì suǒ chū, zhēnwěi chénxīn, bìng gè yǒu fǎ — As to the places of earth from which medicines come, true and false, old and new, each has its own law.**」

——*Shénnóng Běncǎo Jīng · Xùlì* (*The Divine Farmer's Classic of Materia Medica · Prefatory Remarks*)

This is the **fount of the theory of "gēn dì yào cái (道地药材, genuine regional medicinal materials)"**.
The later theory of **"dào-dì yàocái (道地药材, genuine regional medicinals)"**,
the **"Four Great Huái Medicines (sì dà huái yào, 四大怀药)"** (**Huái dìhuáng (rehmannia, 怀地黄), Huái shānyào (Chinese yam, 怀山药), Huái júhuā (chrysanthemum, 怀菊花), Huái niúxī (achyranthes, 怀牛膝)**,
produced in **Huáiqìng Prefecture, Hénan** — present-day **Jiāozuò** and its surroundings),
and the **"Eight Zhe Medicines (zhè bā wèi, 浙八味)"** (**Zhè Báizhú (white atractylodes), Báisháo (white peony), Bèimǔ (fritillaria), Xuánshēn (scrophularia), Màidōng (ophiopogon), Yùjīn (curcuma), Yánhúsuǒ (corydalis), Júhuā (chrysanthemum) of Zhejiang**),
**all trace back to this source**.

### 7. Historical Status and Lineage of Transmission

The ***Běnjīng*** is **the earliest extant monograph of materia medica**.
The original book **was already lost by the Táng dynasty**,
and its contents have been **preserved by later reconstructions from quotations (jīyì běn, 辑佚本)**.

**Important lines of transmission**:

- **The *Běncǎo Jīng Jízhù* (《本草经集注》, Collected Annotations on the Classic of Materia Medica)** — **Táo Hóngjǐng (陶弘景)** of the **Southern Dynasties (c. 456–536)** **edited and annotated** it; it recorded **730 medicinal substances** and was the first to create the **"zhūyào tōngyòng (诸药通用, general use of medicines)"** classification
- **The *Xīnxiū Běncǎo* (《新修本草》, Newly Revised Materia Medica)** — compiled by **Sū Jìng (苏敬)** and others of the Táng, **promulgated in 659 CE**, the **world's first national pharmacopoeia**
- **The *Jīngshǐ Zhènglèi Bèijí Běncǎo* (《经史证类备急本草》, Materia Medica Arranged by Classics and Histories, for Urgent Use)** — by **Táng Shènwēi (唐慎微)** of the Sòng, **1108 CE**, gathering the great achievements of all his predecessors
- **The *Běncǎo Gāngmù* (《本草纲目》, Compendium of Materia Medica)** — by **Lǐ Shízhēn (李时珍)** of the Míng, **completed in 1578**, recording **1,892 medicinal substances**, the **great compendium of materia medica**

In **2011** (together with the *Huángdì Nèijīng*, the *Běncǎo Gāngmù* and others)
it was inscribed by **UNESCO on the Memory of the World Register** (see the previous section).

## VI. The Internal Unity of the Four Classics — "Theory, Method, Formula and Medicine" All Complete

The four classics are **not isolated from one another**,
but **four great pillars** — **theory, method, formula and medicine (lǐ-fǎ-fāng-yào, 理法方药)** — **standing together**,
**as one thread running through them**.

### 1. The *Nèijīng* Sets Up Theory

The **Nèijīng** answers **"why"** —
**Yin-Yang, the five elements, visceral manifestation and the meridians, cause of disease and mechanism of disease, therapeutic principles and methods**,
**constructing the theoretical system of Chinese medicine**.
Without the *Nèijīng*, **Chinese medicine would have no theoretical root**.

### 2. The *Nánjīng* Explains Theory

The **Nánjīng** answers **the difficulties left unclarified in the *Nèijīng*** —
**taking the cùnkǒu alone, left kidney and right mìngmén, yíng-suí tonifying and draining, the five-shū points**,
**supplementing**, **deepening** and **concretising** the theory of the *Nèijīng*.
Without the *Nánjīng*, **much of the *Nèijīng*'s theory would be difficult to put into practice**.

### 3. The *Běnjīng* Sets Up Medicines

The **Běnjīng** answers **"with what"** —
**365 medicinal substances, three-grade classification, four qì and five flavours, seven relations of combination**,
**constructing the system of Chinese pharmacology**.
Without the *Běnjīng*, **Chinese medicine would have no medicines to use**.

### 4. The *Shānghán* Sets Up Method

The **Shānghán** answers **"how to use"** —
**six-channel pattern differentiation, 397 methods, 113 formulas, 151 medicinal substances**,
**constructing the clinical-treatment system**.
Without the *Shānghán*, **Chinese medicine would have no method to follow**.

## VII. The Modern Significance of the Four Classics

The four classics are **by no means "antiques"** —

### 1. Contemporary Chinese-Medicine Education Still Has the Four Classics at Its Root

In the **"Standards for Undergraduate Chinese-Medicine Education"** issued by the **National Administration of Traditional Chinese Medicine (国家中医药管理局)**,

### 2. Tú Yōuyōu and Artemisinin — The Classics Inspired a Nobel Prize

In **2015**, the **Chinese pharmacologist Tú Yōuyōu (屠呦呦)** was awarded the **Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine**
**"for her discoveries concerning a novel therapy against malaria"**.
In her **Nobel lecture**, she stated clearly that her inspiration came from —

> 「**Qīnghāo yī wò, yǐ shuǐ èr shēng zì, jiǎo qǔ zhī jìn fú zhī — A handful of qīnghāo (sweet wormwood), soaked in two shēng of water, wring out the juice and drink it all.**」

——Eastern Jìn · Gě Hóng, *Zhǒuhòu Bèijí Fāng · Zhì Hánrè Zhū Nüè Fāng* (*A Handbook of Formulas for Emergencies, Kept Up One's Sleeve · Formulas for Treating the Various Malarias of Cold and Heat*) (c. 340 CE)

(The **Zhǒuhòu Bèijí Fāng** itself is **not one of the four classics**, but it shares **the same source as the Běnjīng in the Chinese-classical tradition**.)

It was precisely the **"wring out the juice (jiǎo qǔ zhī, 绞取之)"** (i.e. **low-temperature extraction**) recorded by **Gě Hóng** that inspired **Tú Yōuyōu** to switch to **ether-based low-temperature extraction**,
and on **4 October 1971** she obtained **Sample No. 191** —
**100% inhibition of the malaria parasite (nüèyuánchóng, 疟原虫)**,
and so **artemisinin (qīnghāosù, 青蒿素, artemisinin)** was discovered.
**Artemisinin and its derivatives have since saved millions of malaria patients worldwide**,
and **Tú Yōuyōu thereby became the first scientist from mainland China to receive a Nobel Prize in the natural sciences**.
(Source: **nobelprize.org** biography of Tú Yōuyōu)
